I am an Associate Professor in the Department of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science at UC Irvine, where I lead the HPCForge lab. Before joining UCI, I was a Research Scientist at MIT CSAIL. I received my Ph.D. from Georgia Tech in 2013, advised by Richard Vuduc.

Here is some information for prospective students and visitors.

I am always looking for motivated PhD students and postdocs interested in scientific machine learning and high-performance computing. Please email your CV and one representative publication (if any) to amowli [at] uci [dot] edu.

Prospective PhD students: please apply through the UCI EECS system and list me as a potential advisor. There is no need to contact me separately unless you have a specific research question to discuss.

Research

My group works at the intersection of scientific machine learning and high-performance computing — building systems that make large-scale scientific simulation faster, more generalizable, and deployable in the real world. A central question we ask: what does it take to replace or accelerate a physics simulation with a learned model, and how do we scale that to the hardest problems in science?

We approach this from two directions simultaneously. On the learning side, we develop neural PDE solvers, transformer surrogates, and generalist models that learn shared physical structure across conditions and domains. On the systems side, we build the HPC infrastructure — scalable algorithms, domain decomposition strategies, and GPU kernels — needed to make training and inference practical at scale. Representative projects include BubbleML, Bubbleformer, ScalablePINNs, and Mosaic Flows.

We simultaneously explore these ideas across scientific domains:

  • thermal-fluids and multiphase flow — boiling, nucleation, and phase change heat transfer, as in BubbleML and Bubbleformer;
  • neural solvers for PDEs on complex and unseen domains, as in Mosaic Flows and ScalablePINNs;
  • turbulent flow simulation and super-resolution with transfer learning, as in CFDNet and SURFNet;
  • scalable parallel algorithms and communication for MPI+Threads and GPU-accelerated systems.
